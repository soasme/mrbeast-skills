# Critical Components: World's Largest Swimming Pool Challenge

## What Is a Critical Component?

A critical component is anything that — if it fails — means you have no video. For this production, three components meet that threshold immediately.

---

## Critical Component 1: The Olympic-Size Facility

**Why it's critical:** Without the pool, you have nowhere to film. No facility = no video, full stop. This is the single most important logistics lock you need.

**How to track and protect it:**

- **Lock it immediately** — get a signed contract, not a verbal agreement. Verify it covers the full shoot window.
- **Negotiate a backup day** — when you secure the facility, build a one-day extension option into the contract. A pool that gives you a backup day beats a marginally better pool that won't. The backup day exists for weather delays, equipment failure, or a contestant no-show situation.
- **Daily check-in** — assign one person who owns this relationship. They check in with the facility contact daily in the final two weeks before the shoot. Not weekly. Daily.
- **Get video** — have someone physically scout the pool on video, not just send you photos. One person's photos are not a team's mental model. You need to see depth, clearance above water, access points, where power is, where equipment can be staged.
- **Confirm closer to shoot day** — re-confirm the booking 72 hours before shoot and again morning of. Facilities can double-book, have emergencies, or pull access.
- **Escalation rule** — if anything changes with the facility's availability, escalate to leadership immediately. Not in a week. That day.

---

## Critical Component 2: Custom Floating Obstacles

**Why it's critical:** The floating obstacles are almost certainly the thumbnail visual. They're what makes this video "World's Largest" — without them, you have a swimming video, not a challenge video. If the thumbnail visual doesn't exist, you don't have a coherent video.

**How to track and protect it:**

- **Set a deadline earlier than you need it** — if shoot is in 3 weeks, you need the obstacles completed and inspected 1 week before shoot. Build your deadline backward from there, not forward from today.
- **Daily check-ins with the contractor** — whoever is building these gets a daily check-in. Ask for video updates of work in progress. Not text updates. Video. Color correct? Scale correct? Structural integrity looks right? These things can only be verified visually.
- **Video proof at each milestone** — you need to see the obstacles at rough build stage, at finishing stage, and fully completed. "It looks great, almost done" from a contractor means nothing. Video evidence means something.
- **Never trust standard shipping** — if the obstacles need to be transported, pay for hand-delivery or confirm the contractor is transporting them directly. Standard shipping is never acceptable for a critical prop. One lost or damaged obstacle and you have no thumbnail.
- **Build a backup** — identify a simpler version of the obstacle concept that could be fabricated quickly with local materials if the main build falls through. It doesn't have to be as impressive — it has to exist. A 50/50 critical component is a 50/50 video. Build the backup before the crisis.
- **Inspect in person** — at some point before shoot day, someone from the production team sees these obstacles in person or in a live video call, not just from contractor-submitted photos.

---

## Critical Component 3: 20 Contestants

**Why it's critical:** The video title and premise promise 20 contestants. If only 10 show up, you either have no video or a fundamentally different video than you planned. Contestant logistics are a classic failure point because each one is an independent risk.

**How to track and protect it:**

- **Over-recruit** — sign up 30 contestants for 20 spots. Assume 5-6 won't show. If more show up than expected, that's a good problem to solve.
- **Daily communication in the final week** — each contestant should receive at least one direct outreach per day in the final week. Not a group message. Direct confirmation.
- **Locked commitment, not casual confirmation** — a contestant saying "yeah I'll be there" is not the same as a contestant with a confirmed time, confirmed transportation, and confirmed understanding of requirements. Get the logistics in writing.
- **Backup contestants on standby** — maintain a list of 5-10 alternates who are fully briefed, ready to show up same-day if primary contestants drop out. These alternates need to know they're on standby, not just passively on a list.
- **Morning-of headcount protocol** — assign someone to track contestant arrivals in real time on shoot day. If you're at 17 confirmed and 3 haven't checked in by 30 minutes before shoot, that person is calling those 3 and activating alternates simultaneously.
- **Check for conflicts early** — confirm contestants haven't double-booked or have life events (travel, illness, family issues) that could create day-of cancellations. Ask about this a week out, not the day before.

---

## Summary: What Happens If Each Fails

| Component | If it fails |
|---|---|
| Facility | No video. Everything else is irrelevant. |
| Floating obstacles | No thumbnail visual. Video concept collapses. |
| 20 contestants | Video title is wrong, premise is broken, or you film with half the energy/spectacle. |

---

## Pre-Shoot Verification Checklist

- [ ] Facility contract signed with backup day option confirmed
- [ ] Facility scouted on video by production team
- [ ] Obstacle contractor delivering daily video updates
- [ ] Obstacles verified for color, scale, and structural integrity at least 1 week before shoot
- [ ] Backup obstacle plan exists and is actionable
- [ ] 30+ contestants recruited for 20 slots
- [ ] 5-10 alternates on confirmed standby
- [ ] Daily contestant outreach in final week
- [ ] All three components have a named person responsible for them
- [ ] Leadership knows status of all three critical components at all times
