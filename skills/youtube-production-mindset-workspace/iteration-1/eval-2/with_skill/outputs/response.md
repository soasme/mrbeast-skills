# Contractor Accountability: Giant Prop Castle

## Short Answer: No, This Is Not Okay

Going five days without contact from a contractor building a critical prop is not a neutral situation — it is an active risk. You are experiencing the classic production failure pattern: **dump-and-forget**. You assigned the work, gave them a deadline, and walked away. That is not contractor management. That is hoping.

If the castle falls through and you didn't check in daily, that failure is on you — not on the contractor.

---

## What You Should Be Doing Instead

### 1. Reach Out Today — Not Tomorrow

Contact the contractor right now. Call, don't text. You need to understand where they are in the build, what they've completed, and what problems (if any) they've already encountered. Five days of silence can mean they're heads-down building, or it can mean they've hit a materials problem and didn't want to bother you. You need to know which.

### 2. Establish Daily Check-Ins Starting Immediately

From this point forward, you have a daily check-in with this contractor. Not weekly. Not "whenever there's an update." Daily. Here's what that looks like:

- A short call or message every day confirming status
- A specific question: "What did you finish today? What are you working on tomorrow?"
- A request for **video updates of the work in progress** — not photos, not text updates. Video. You need to see the castle being built. Is the scale right? Is the color right? Are the materials what you expected? These things cannot be confirmed from a text message.

### 3. Set an Internal Deadline Earlier Than Your Actual Shoot Date

Your contractor knows the shoot is in 3 weeks. That means in their mind, they have 3 weeks. You need to reset that expectation immediately.

The internal deadline you give them should be **1 week before the actual shoot** — or at minimum 5 days before. This gives you time to:
- Inspect the finished prop and identify problems
- Request changes if scale, color, or materials are wrong
- Handle transportation and staging logistics
- Build a replacement or backup if the prop is unusable

If you set the deadline at shoot day, you have no recovery window.

### 4. Build a Backup Option in Parallel

If the castle is the critical visual for this video — if it appears in the thumbnail or is the central prop the creator specifically loves — then you need to start building a backup right now.

The backup doesn't need to be identical. It needs to be enough to make the video work if the primary castle falls through. That could be:
- A simpler castle structure from a local prop rental company
- A different visual treatment of the castle concept that can be executed quickly
- A version the production team could fabricate in-house if needed

A 50/50 critical prop is a 50/50 video. Build the backup before you need it, not after the crisis.

### 5. Confirm the Material, Scale, and Color Details Now

Don't assume the contractor's interpretation of "giant castle" matches yours. In the same call where you re-establish contact, confirm:
- The exact dimensions (height, width, footprint)
- The materials being used
- The color palette
- Any structural requirements (does it need to support weight? be waterproof? have doors that open?)

If these details haven't been confirmed in writing, confirm them now and send a follow-up summary via text or email that they acknowledge. An email that isn't acknowledged hasn't landed.

---

## Why Silence Is a Risk, Not Reassurance

New producers sometimes interpret contractor silence as a good sign — "they must be busy building, no news is good news." This is backwards.

In production, no news means no visibility. You have zero information about:
- Whether they started
- Whether they've hit a problem they haven't told you about
- Whether their interpretation of the brief matches yours
- Whether they'll actually finish on time

You cannot manage what you cannot see. The purpose of daily check-ins and video updates is not to micromanage — it's to surface problems early enough that you can fix them. A problem discovered two weeks before shoot is solvable. The same problem discovered the day before shoot is a crisis.

---

## The Accountability Rule

If the contractor fails to deliver and you didn't check in daily, that's on you — not them. The contractor took the job. You own the production outcome. The check-in process is how you protect the production from human unpredictability.

---

## Immediate Action Items

1. Call the contractor today
2. Get a video update of current progress
3. Confirm all material, scale, and color details
4. Set an internal completion deadline 5-7 days before the shoot
5. Schedule daily check-ins for the remainder of the build
6. Start identifying a backup prop option in parallel
7. If the castle is a critical component, escalate current status to leadership now
