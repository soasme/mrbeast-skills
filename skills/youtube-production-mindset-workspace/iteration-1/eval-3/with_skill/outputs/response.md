# Shoot Day Crisis: Pyrotechnics Won't Work — 4 Hours Left

## Immediate Response: Act in the First 5 Minutes

Do not spend the next hour troubleshooting with the FX team while the clock runs out. The moment it's clear the pyrotechnics are not working, do three things simultaneously:

### 1. Escalate to Leadership Immediately

This is not a "figure it out and report back" situation. Get on the phone with the director and executive producer right now — not in 30 minutes, not after another attempt. They need to know immediately because:
- They may have resources or contacts you don't
- Decisions about restructuring the shoot require their authority
- "I didn't tell anyone until it was too late" is a production failure, not a production save

The communication form for a critical component in jeopardy is not a text. It's a call, ideally with multiple people on the line.

### 2. Ask: Can This Failure Become Content?

Before you try to recreate the planned scene, stop and ask this question out loud to the room: **"Can the pyrotechnics failing be the funniest moment in the video?"**

Production failures on set, when handled with the right energy, regularly become the best moments in a video. The FX team frantically trying to make it work, the creator's genuine reaction to the malfunction, a comedic "we tried" sequence — these are real, unscripted, and often more compelling than a polished effect that works perfectly. Don't waste the failure. Capture it.

### 3. Explore All Alternatives in Parallel — Don't Wait for One Option to Fail Before Trying the Next

While leadership is being briefed and the failure-as-content angle is being considered, run these simultaneously:

**Contact other FX vendors or specialists immediately.** "No" from the current team is not the final answer. Call every FX contact in the network right now. Is there another company that could get someone on-site in the next 2 hours? Is there a simpler practical solution — smoke machines, confetti cannons, large LED flash rigs — that could approximate the visual?

**Ask if the scene can be filmed differently.** Does the finale require actual pyrotechnics, or does it require an impressive, high-energy visual climax? Could the same shot work with a different practical effect? Color gels? A different camera angle that makes a simpler effect look more dramatic?

**Assess what you actually need vs. what you planned.** Sometimes the planned approach and the necessary outcome are different things. The finale needs to land emotionally — does it specifically require fire, or does it require scale and surprise?

---

## The Question You Should Also Be Asking Right Now

**Why wasn't there a backup plan for this?**

If pyrotechnics were a critical component of this video — and if they appear in the thumbnail or are a creator-approved "must have" — then the pre-production process should have included:
- A backup FX approach identified and pre-vetted before shoot day
- The FX team required to do a successful test run before shoot day, not troubleshoot for the first time on set
- A protocol for exactly this scenario

The absence of a backup is a production planning failure. That doesn't mean you compound it by freezing — you move fast with what you have. But after the shoot, this becomes a post-mortem item.

## In the Next 4 Hours: Priority Order

| Time | Action |
|---|---|
| Now | Call leadership, alert director. Do not delay. |
| Now | Tell camera team: capture the FX team's troubleshooting — this may become content |
| Next 15 min | Call every alternative FX contact available |
| Next 15 min | Identify practical alternative visual approaches |
| Next 30 min | Decision point: pursue alternative FX, pivot to different climax, or lean into the failure as content |
| Ongoing | Keep leadership informed every 30 minutes until resolved |

---

## What Not to Do

- **Do not just skip the finale scene** without exploring every option first. Skipping a planned scene because something didn't work — without exhausting alternatives — is accepting a worse video without a fight.
- **Do not keep troubleshooting with only the original FX team** while the clock runs out. Parallel paths are required.
- **Do not wait to escalate** until you've "tried everything." Escalate first, try things simultaneously.

---

## The Underlying Principle

"No" from the pyrotechnics team is not the final answer. Work around it, find a different vendor, change the approach, or make the failure itself the story. The production mindset is to push through no — exhaust every avenue before accepting that the scene is lost.

And remember: the best content is often unplanned. A pyrotechnics failure handled with creativity and genuine energy might be more memorable than a clean special effect that worked exactly as planned.
