# Chef Kitchen Swap: Format Design

## Core Format: Outcome-Unknown (Chase/Parallel Tracks)

The structural problem with a "we swapped kitchens for a week" video is that it has no natural endpoint unless you engineer one. Seven days of footage without a clear question is just a vlog. The format has to answer: **what question drives viewers to the end?**

The answer: **Who performs worse?** (or: Who actually produces better food in an unfamiliar environment?)

This creates an open-loop question in the first 60 seconds. Viewers pick sides, form predictions, and must watch to see if they're right.

---

## The Open-Loop Hook

Open with a direct challenge. In the first 60 seconds, establish:

1. "I'm going to cook in [Chef's name]'s Michelin-star kitchen for a week."
2. "They're going to cook in my home kitchen for a week."
3. "At the end, real food critics will taste both kitchens' dishes — blind. They won't know which is which."
4. "Whoever gets the better scores wins."

You have now planted a question that cannot be answered until the final minutes: *who wins the blind taste test?*

Do NOT hint at the result. Do not say "what happened next shocked everyone" or show reaction clips from the end. The payoff's power depends entirely on the outcome remaining unknown.

---

## Story Structure: Two Parallel Tracks

The body of the video runs two parallel storylines that intercut:

**Track A: You in the Michelin kitchen**
- Show Day 1 disorientation — you don't know where anything is, staff doesn't know how to help you
- Show an early failure (burned dish, wrong technique, a tasting that goes badly)
- Show gradual adaptation — you figure out one or two things
- Build to your "best dish" attempt near the end of the week

**Track B: The chef in your home kitchen**
- Show Day 1 humiliation — commercial chef can't find anything, is horrified by the equipment
- Show an early failure (overcooked on a cheap stove, wrong pans, no mise en place setup)
- Show the chef improvising — they may adapt faster than you, or they may struggle worse
- Build to their "best dish" attempt

**Critical:** Show genuine failures from BOTH tracks early. If viewers think one side is obviously winning early, they stop watching. The race needs to feel competitive or at minimum uncertain.

The two tracks should converge roughly 70% through the video, where both kitchens are preparing their final dishes for the judging moment.

---

## The Payoff

The payoff is a **blind taste test judged by credible external evaluators** — not by you or the chef (you're both biased and invested). Options:

- **Professional food critics**: Ideally people who know the chef by reputation but cannot identify their style blind
- **Michelin restaurant regular customers**: Real patrons who ate at the restaurant before and have a baseline expectation
- **Both**: Show the scores from multiple groups

The judging scene structure:
1. Show the dishes being plated without identification
2. Judges taste and score without knowing which kitchen produced what
3. Scores are announced
4. Reveal which kitchen made which dish — capture the judges' reactions to the surprise
5. Cut immediately after the reveal and reaction. Do not add commentary.

This is the payoff. The video ends here.

---

## What to Avoid

- **Day 1, Day 2, Day 3 structure**: This is a vlog structure, not a format. It creates no tension. Use it only as an internal reference ("by Day 3 I had figured out...") but don't make each day a separate beat.
- **Revealing the outcome early**: Any hint of who performed better before the blind taste test kills the format.
- **Letting the chef be the judge**: The credibility of the payoff requires external evaluation.
- **Adding a winner's speech or recap**: The video ends with the payoff reaction, not a summary.

---

## Format Classification

This is primarily a **Chase/Outcome-Unknown format** with parallel tracks. The "chase" is the competition: both parties are racing toward an outcome that the viewer cannot predict. The parallel structure is borrowed from the survival/survival format, but the payoff is a hard competitive resolution rather than a duration milestone.

If you wanted to add a stair-stepping layer, you could have multiple judging moments across the week (Wednesday tasting, Friday tasting, final Sunday tasting) — each tasting escalates in stakes and formality.
