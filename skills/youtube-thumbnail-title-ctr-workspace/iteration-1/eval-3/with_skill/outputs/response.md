# CTR Diagnosis: "Helping Homeless People Find Homes"

## Diagnosis: Why CTR Is Low

### Problem 1: The Title Has No Extremity or Scale

"Helping Homeless People Find Homes" is generic and descriptive. It tells you what the video is about, but nothing about it is extreme, unusual, or superlative. This exact title could describe a news segment, a nonprofit ad, or a 2-minute Instagram reel. There's no scale — how many people? What did it cost? What happened?

**The extremity rule applies here:** Neutral language performs poorly. The title needs a number, an outcome, or a scale indicator to create curiosity.

What's missing:
- No number ("100 people," "50 families")
- No dollar amount ("$500,000 worth of housing")
- No outcome hook ("...and what happened next")
- No emotional peak ("...and they cried")
- No stakes or challenge framing

### Problem 2: The Thumbnail Shows a Conversation, Not a Payoff

A creator talking to a person on a street corner is the most generic possible image for this content. Every news story, every documentary, every charity video uses this exact shot. It communicates: "here is a problem being discussed." It does not communicate: "here is something remarkable you've never seen."

**The thumbnail has no visual hook because:**
1. It shows the process/problem, not the transformation or outcome
2. There is no scale visible (one person doesn't suggest "many people helped")
3. There is no emotional peak moment
4. It blends into every other "person talking on a street" thumbnail
5. No implied motion or stakes — nothing looks like it's about to happen

---

## Rewritten Title Options

### Option 1 (Recommended)
**"I Got 100 Homeless People Off The Streets"**

- Number creates scale (100 = substantial, real effort)
- "Got Off The Streets" is a clear outcome — result, not process
- Implies a before/after transformation
- A viewer who cares about this issue will click immediately; curious viewers want to know how

### Option 2
**"I Paid For Housing For Every Homeless Person In [City]"**

- "Every" signals completeness and extreme commitment
- Geographic specificity adds credibility and stakes
- Raises the question: how much did that cost? Was it really everyone?

### Option 3
**"I Gave $1,000,000 To Homeless Families — Here's What Happened"**

- Dollar amount creates scale and stakes
- "Here's What Happened" opens a loop — outcome unknown, must watch
- Strong if the actual spend is in that range; adjust number to match reality

### Option 4
**"I Helped 50 Homeless Families Find Their First Home"**

- "First Home" adds emotional weight — it implies a life-changing moment, not just temporary shelter
- Number (50) creates scale
- More emotionally resonant than options focused purely on money

**Most accurate to content (assuming the scale matches):** Option 1 or 4, depending on whether numbers are available.

## Rewritten Thumbnail Concept

**The current thumbnail shows the wrong moment.** A conversation is process. The thumbnail should show the payoff.

### Recommended Thumbnail
**Show the transformation or the scale of impact:**

**Concept A — Scale:**
A wide shot or montage showing many people outside new homes or housing units, arms raised, huge smiles. The creator stands among them. This immediately communicates "many people, big outcome."

**Concept B — Emotional Peak:**
A single powerful moment: a homeless person seeing their new home for the first time, face showing shock and tears. The creator stands beside them. Caption-sized text: "100 FAMILIES" in bold.

**Concept C — Before/After:**
Split screen: left side shows a person on the street with their belongings; right side shows that same person in front of a home, smiling. The contrast tells the whole story in 0.5 seconds.

**Why this works:**
1. One dominant visual — the transformation, not the conversation
2. Human reaction — genuine emotion drives clicks on philanthropic content
3. Scale implied — text or multiple people suggest this is bigger than one person
4. Promises the outcome — viewer knows this story has a resolution

---

## The Content Contract Caveat

**The rewrite must not misrepresent the content.**

If the creator helped 5 people, the title cannot say "100 Families." If no one got permanent housing, the title cannot claim they "Got Off The Streets" permanently.

The thumbnail and title make a promise to the viewer. If the viewer clicks expecting 100 people and sees 3, they click away immediately — causing YouTube's algorithm to penalize the video further. The rewrite must be the most extreme *true* version of what happened.

**Rule:** Use the actual numbers and actual outcomes. If the real story is impressive, these rewrites will work. If the video overstates what happened, the CTR boost will be short-lived and hurt the channel.
