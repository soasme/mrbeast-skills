# CTR Diagnosis: Homeless Video

## Why the CTR Is Low

### The Title Problem

"Helping Homeless People Find Homes" is too generic. It reads like a news headline or a charity campaign tagline — not a YouTube video that promises something remarkable. The issues:

- **No scale or specificity:** How many people? What outcome? This could describe helping 1 person or 1,000.
- **No emotional hook:** "Helping" is a process word. It doesn't tell viewers what they'll see or feel.
- **Competes with everything similar:** This title matches a thousand nonprofit videos, documentaries, and news segments. There's nothing that signals this video specifically is worth clicking.

### The Thumbnail Problem

A creator talking to a person on a street corner is the default image for "homelessness content." It's:
- A conversation, not a climax
- Familiar and forgettable — exactly what viewers scroll past
- Not promising anything — it shows the problem, not any resolution or spectacle

There's no visual reason to think this video is different from any other video about homelessness.

---

## Rewritten Title Options

**Option 1 (Recommended):**
"I Got 100 Homeless People Off The Streets"

This works because it leads with a specific number (scale), uses a clear outcome verb ("got off the streets"), and promises a result rather than a process.

**Option 2:**
"I Paid To House 50 Homeless Families For A Year"

The time duration ("For A Year") adds commitment and cost to the story. It implies both financial scale and long-term impact.

**Option 3:**
"$500,000 To End Homelessness In One City"

The dollar amount immediately signals scale. "End Homelessness" is bold and extreme — viewers want to see if it actually worked.

*Note: Use the real numbers from the video. The title must reflect what actually happened — use the most extreme true version of events.*

---

## Rewritten Thumbnail Concept

Replace the conversation scene with the outcome or transformation:

**Option A — The Reveal Moment:**
Show a formerly homeless person opening the door to their new home for the first time, face filled with emotion. The creator stands nearby. This is the emotional payoff — the moment the video was made to capture.

**Option B — Scale Shot:**
A wide shot showing many people standing outside housing (an apartment building, a row of homes), with the creator in the frame. Overlaid text: "100 PEOPLE" or the relevant number. This communicates the size of the effort immediately.

**Option C — Before/After:**
Split the thumbnail: left panel shows someone sleeping on the street, right panel shows that same person smiling inside a home. The contrast is immediate and emotionally powerful.

**Key principle:** The thumbnail should show the payoff, not the setup. Viewers click on resolutions, not conversations.

---

## Important Note on Accuracy

Any rewrite must stay truthful to the video's content. If the creator helped 10 people, the title should reflect 10 people (or a number close to it). Thumbnails showing large groups must match what's actually in the video. Misleading titles and thumbnails generate clicks but cause viewers to leave quickly, which signals YouTube to stop recommending the video — making the CTR problem worse, not better.

The goal is the most compelling *accurate* version of the story.
