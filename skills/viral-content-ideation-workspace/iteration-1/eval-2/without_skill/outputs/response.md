# Teaching Kids Coding on a $5,000 Budget

The creator wants to make a video about teaching kids to code but thinks they need $50,000. Here's how to make it work with $5,000:

## Budget Breakdown

You don't need $50,000. Here's what $5,000 can buy:

- **Equipment:** Use existing equipment or rent basics ($500)
- **Location:** Use a library, school computer lab, or community center — often free or low cost ($0–$200)
- **Participants:** Find kids through local schools, coding clubs, or online communities (free)
- **Prize/Incentive:** Offer a modest prize like a laptop or gaming setup ($800–$1,500)
- **Miscellaneous:** Food, travel, extras ($500)

**Total: ~$3,000–$3,500, leaving buffer room**

## Content Suggestions

1. **Find interesting kids:** Seek out kids who are already passionate about coding — their enthusiasm and knowledge will create naturally compelling content.

2. **Create a mini hackathon:** Give kids 24 hours to build an app or game. Document the process, the struggles, the breakthroughs.

3. **Add a guest/mentor:** Find a developer willing to volunteer their time as a mentor or judge. Many professionals will do this for free for a good cause.

4. **Simple challenge format:** Give kids a specific coding challenge and see who can solve it fastest or most creatively.

## Key Advice

The video doesn't need expensive equipment or sets to be good. What makes coding content compelling is:
- Real kids having genuine reactions
- Clear wins and failures
- An understandable challenge the audience can follow

Focus on storytelling over production value. A genuine moment of a kid finally getting their code to work is more compelling than any fancy set.
