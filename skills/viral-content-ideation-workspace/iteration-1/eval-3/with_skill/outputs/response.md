# Idea Evaluation: Rubric Scoring and Recommendation

## The 5-Dimension Rubric

Scoring each idea 1–5 on: CTR potential, Simplicity, Wow factor, Emotional investment, Format fit.

---

## Idea A: "I trained for 1 year with an Olympic swimming coach and then competed against the coach"

| Dimension | Score | Reasoning |
|---|---|---|
| **CTR potential** | 2 | The thumbnail problem: how do you show "1 year of training" in one image? The premise requires context that a thumbnail can't deliver. A bored teenager scrolling past sees "guy racing in a pool" — not compelling without the story. |
| **Simplicity** | 3 | Premise is understandable but requires 2–3 sentences to fully explain: who trained, with whom, for how long, then competing against that same person. Not quite 6-word territory. |
| **Wow factor** | 2 | The wow moment — if it lands — is the race itself. But the creator racing the coach is only surprising if the creator *wins*, and that's not guaranteed. The format gambles on an outcome it can't control. No single scene is screenshot-worthy regardless of result. |
| **Emotional investment** | 5 | This is the idea's strongest dimension. A year of training, a clear underdog, a specific rival who also has stakes — viewers genuinely want to know the outcome. The journey footage creates attachment. |
| **Format fit** | 4 | Strong built-in reason to watch to the end: will they win or lose? The training montage + race payoff is a proven format. But it requires excellent execution to maintain pacing across 1 year of content compression. |
| **Total** | **16/25** | Below the 20+ threshold for strong concepts. Needs significant reworking. |

---

## Idea B: "I built a swimming pool out of 50,000 water balloons"

| Dimension | Score | Reasoning |
|---|---|---|
| **CTR potential** | 5 | Instant thumbnail: a pool-shaped structure made entirely of water balloons, person submerged. One image communicates the entire premise. A bored teenager clicks immediately — the visual is bizarre and irresistible. |
| **Simplicity** | 5 | "Pool made of 50,000 water balloons" — 7 words, understood by a 10-year-old globally. Perfect premise simplicity. |
| **Wow factor** | 4 | The moment the water balloons are filled in and someone actually "swims" (sinks, really) into it is the screenshot moment. The scale of 50,000 balloons assembled is visually staggering. Only a creator with serious resources and crew can pull this off at scale. |
| **Emotional investment** | 2 | The primary weakness: there's no rooting interest, no opponent, no stakes. Viewers are entertained but not invested. When the balloons pop, what did we care about? Adding a challenge layer — "can I stay inside for 24 hours?" or "last person in wins $10,000" — would solve this entirely. |
| **Format fit** | 3 | The build itself is interesting but the video risks ending too early: you build it, you get in, balloons pop. There's no built-in reason to watch until minute 15. Needs a challenge or competition layer to extend engagement. |
| **Total** | **19/25** | Close to the 20+ threshold. One structural addition (a challenge/stakes layer) pushes this to 22+. |

---

## Recommendation: Produce Idea B (with one modification)

**Idea B is the stronger concept.** Its CTR, simplicity, and wow factor scores are dramatically higher — and those dimensions determine whether the video gets *seen* at all. A video with great emotional investment that nobody clicks on doesn't help anyone.

**Idea A's fatal flaw** is the thumbnail problem. The premise requires the viewer to already understand the backstory before it's interesting. You cannot build a thumbnail around "1 year of training." This is a core CTR problem that can't be fixed without fundamentally changing the concept.

**Idea B's fixable weakness** is emotional investment. The solution is simple and costs almost nothing: add a challenge layer.

**Upgraded Idea B:** "I Built A Pool From 50,000 Water Balloons — Last Person Inside Wins $20,000." Now you have CTR (the visual), a wow factor (the build), AND stakes (the competition). Score jumps to approximately 23/25.

---

## How to Improve the Weaker Idea (Idea A)

If you want to develop Idea A instead, it needs a structural reframe:

1. **Add a specific stakes moment:** "If I beat the Olympic coach, I win $1,000,000 — if I lose, I donate $100,000 to charity." Now the outcome matters financially and the stakes are real regardless of who wins.

2. **Reframe the thumbnail:** Show the creator and the Olympic coach face-to-face in the water, side by side, the moment before the race. Now it's a face-off thumbnail — instantly understood, high CTR.

3. **Sharpen the title:** "I Trained 1 Year to Beat An Olympic Coach" — 8 words, understandable, creates a clear underdog premise.

These changes push Idea A from 16 to approximately 21/25 — above the production threshold.

---

## Final Verdict

**Produce Idea B now** with the $20,000 challenge layer added. Consider developing the upgraded version of Idea A as a future video once the channel has established strong emotional investment content as a proven format.
