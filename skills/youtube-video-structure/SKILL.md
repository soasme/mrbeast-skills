# YouTube Video Structure

## The Core Principle

YouTube's retention graphs show the exact second each viewer leaves. The graph drops steeply at the start of every video and flattens once viewers are emotionally invested. Your job is to architect each minute to slow that drop and build investment.

**The average high-performing video runs ~13 minutes.** Structure every minute intentionally.

## The Minute-by-Minute Blueprint

### Minute 0–1: The Hook (Most Critical)

This is where you lose the most viewers — typically 30–40% of clicks leave in the first minute. Every production decision must serve slowing this drop.

Requirements:
- **Match thumbnail expectations immediately** — the visual from the thumbnail must appear in the first 15 seconds
- **Good lighting** — poor lighting signals low quality before content begins
- **Front-load the most interesting content** — show the scale, the spectacle, the absurdity
- **No slow warmup** — skip the "in today's video we..." preamble

### Minutes 1–3: Transition to Execution

Stop *telling* viewers what they're watching. Start *showing* it's happening.

**Crazy progression tactic:** Compress early story beats. If this is a 7-day survival video, show Days 1, 2, and 3 in the first 3 minutes. The viewer is already emotionally invested in the character's progress and wants to see how far it goes.

**The 3-Minute Re-engagement:** Around minute 3, insert a spectacle moment — something that makes the viewer think "only this channel can do this." This is high-production, impressive, and fits the story. It re-hooks viewers who are starting to get comfortable and might click away.

Examples of re-engagements: bringing in a crane to lift a house into a challenge circle, a celebrity surprise, a massive practical stunt tied to the story.

### Minutes 3–6: Fall in Love with the Story

This is where you win the audience for the whole video. If they're still watching at 6 minutes, most will finish.

Content style:
- Quick scene changes
- Highly stimulating but simple content
- Moments that develop the characters / contestants
- Easy-to-follow story beats — no complex explanation needed here

**The 6-Minute Re-engagement:** Another high-stakes moment, but this one can take slightly more setup. It should push the story into the back half and raise the stakes for the second half of the video.

### Minutes 6–End: Back Half (Lull + Payoff)

Once a viewer is 6 minutes in, they're in a "lull" — watching without consciously deciding to watch. This is where you can:
- Include longer explanation segments
- Handle unexpected/unplanned content that still fits the story
- Show emotional or slow-burn moments that reward investment

**Rules for the back half:**
- Never signal the ending is coming (unless it's to build hype for the prize/payoff)
- No "well, that's it for today" energy mid-video
- Save the biggest payoff for last
- End abruptly after the payoff — no lengthy outro

## Re-Engagement Calibration

| Re-engagement type | Best at | Characteristics |
|---|---|---|
| High spectacle, simple to understand | 3-min mark | Quick, jaw-dropping, no explanation needed |
| Medium spectacle, story-critical | 6-min mark | Takes 30-60 seconds to set up, pushes narrative |
| Emotional payoff / callback | Back half | Rewards viewers who invested in characters |

## The Video Structure Worksheet

When outlining a video, fill in:

```
TITLE/THUMBNAIL: [what the viewer expects]

HOOK (0–60 sec):
- Opening visual (confirms thumbnail): ___
- Most exciting/extreme front-loaded content: ___
- Stakes established: ___

TRANSITION (1–3 min):
- Execution begins: ___
- Crazy progression (compress early beats): ___
- 3-minute re-engagement: ___

STORY BUILD (3–6 min):
- Scene 1: ___
- Scene 2: ___
- Scene 3: ___
- 6-minute re-engagement: ___

BACK HALF (6 min – end):
- Main story beats: ___
- Unexpected content opportunity: ___
- Final payoff: ___
- Ending: [abrupt, after payoff]
```

## Minute-Mark Awareness

Every person working on a video — production, creative, editor — must know what minute of the 13-minute video they are working on. Content decisions are different at minute 2 vs minute 9.

When planning a segment, always tag it:
- What minute does this content live at?
- Does the content match the appropriate energy for that minute?
- Is this the right place for this type of content?

## Common Structural Mistakes

| Mistake | Effect |
|---|---|
| No re-engagement at 3-minute mark | Viewer boredom peak = mass click-off |
| Back half that "signals the end" | Viewers leave early — kills AVD |
| Saving best content for late | Viewers never get there |
| Slow first 60 seconds | 40%+ drop before content begins |
| Complex explanation in minutes 1–3 | Viewer disengages before investment |
| Abrupt payoff in first half | Nothing left to watch for |
