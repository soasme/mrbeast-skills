# Two Brand Deals in a 12-Minute Obstacle Course Competition Video

## Is It Manageable?

Yes — two brand deals in 12 minutes is entirely workable, but placement and execution both need to be correct. Done well, the viewer barely registers the pacing shift. Done wrong, you get two retention craters and both sponsors get worse conversion than they paid for.

---

## Placement Strategy

| Deal | Recommended Placement | Reasoning |
|---|---|---|
| Brand Deal #1 | **Minutes 6–7** | Viewer is past the competition setup, first rounds of action have happened, natural lull before the field narrows |
| Brand Deal #2 | **Minutes 9–10** | After the mid-video stakes escalate, another lull before the final. Viewer is highly invested and will return |

**Critical rules:**
- Neither deal goes before minute 6. The first 5 minutes are entirely off-limits — that's when viewers decide if they're watching the whole thing.
- Do **not** run both deals back-to-back. A viewer who sits through one sponsor segment will tolerate it. Two in a row signals "this is an ad break" and they leave.
- Separate the two deals by at least 2–3 minutes of competition content.

---

## Integration Strategies That Fit the Context

A $500,000 obstacle course with 20 contestants gives you excellent integration angles:

**Deal #1 — Performance/Athletic Brand (energy drink, protein bar, athletic gear)**

This is a natural fit. The obstacle course is physically demanding — a brand that helps contestants perform fits organically.

*Example integration:* Cut to a contestant catching their breath between rounds. Host walks over: "You doing okay? Here — [hands them a product]. This is what I've been using to stay locked in during filming." Show the product being used, not just talked about. 30–45 seconds, then cut back to action.

**Deal #2 — Tech/App/Digital Brand**

Competition formats work well for mobile game or app integrations if the brand allows creativity.

*Example integration:* "While we wait for the next round to set up, [contestant] is going to try to beat my score in [game/app] for a hundred bucks." Turn the sponsor segment into a side bet. The product gets screen time, it's entertaining, and it's tied to the competition stakes.

If the second brand is less entertainment-friendly (insurance, financial product, etc.), fall back to a comedic approach — the host addresses it directly and briefly, with a self-aware joke, and keeps it under 40 seconds.

---

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

**1. Front-loading the sponsors**
Putting either deal in the first 5 minutes punishes early engagement. Viewers came for the $500,000 competition — they'll skip immediately if the video starts feeling like an ad.

**2. Back-to-back sponsor segments**
Two sponsors in a row, even great ones, reads as an ad break. Viewer trust drops and retention craters. Always separate with real competition content.

**3. Scripted reads that pause the competition**
The worst version: competition stops, host addresses camera directly, reads the sponsor out loud, then says "okay back to the competition." This destroys pacing. The integration should feel like it happens *within* the competition world, not in a separate sponsor universe.

**4. Sponsors placed before the action escalates**
In a competition video, the action escalates as contestants get eliminated. The tension builds. Placing a sponsor before the field has narrowed means you're interrupting before the viewer cares. Place sponsors *after* significant eliminations, not before.

**5. Both deals going to brands that aren't thematically connected**
If both sponsors feel random (a mattress brand and a language app), the integrations will feel forced. Try to connect both to physical performance, competition, or the host's personal experience during filming.

---

## Retention Effect

Correctly placed: the retention graph shows two small dips — minor and recoverable. Viewers return to the competition content.

Incorrectly placed (front-loaded or back-to-back): two large craters, reduced average view duration, sponsors get lower conversion on a weaker audience.

The brand gets better ROI from an integrated mid-video placement than from a traditional read, even if the integrated version is shorter.
