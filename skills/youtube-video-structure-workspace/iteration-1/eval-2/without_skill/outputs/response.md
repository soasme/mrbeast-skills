# Structural Review: Submitted Video Outline

## Problems Identified

### 1. The Introduction is Too Slow (Minutes 0–2)

Starting with rules and a sponsor read is risky for viewer retention. Most YouTube viewers decide within the first 30 seconds whether to keep watching. A 2-minute introduction with rule explanations may cause early drop-off before the interesting content begins.

**Recommendation:** Trim the introduction to 30–45 seconds. Move the sponsor mention later in the video (around minute 5–7 is common practice). Open with something visual and exciting that immediately represents the challenge concept.

---

### 2. Set-Building Content May Not Be Engaging (Minutes 2–5)

Showing the set being constructed is production content, not challenge content. Unless the set-building is itself dramatic or impressive, this 3-minute segment may not hold viewer attention.

**Recommendation:** Consider trimming this to 30–60 seconds of interesting highlights, or cutting it entirely and replacing with contestant arrival energy and early challenge setup.

---

### 3. The Challenge Starts Late (Minutes 8–12)

With 15 minutes of total content, starting the actual challenge at minute 8 means viewers spend the first half of the video in setup mode. This is a significant pacing issue — the core "promise" of the video (the challenge itself) is delivered more than halfway through.

**Recommendation:** Find ways to start the challenge earlier, ideally by minute 4–5. The interviews can happen after some initial challenge action, or can be woven into the challenge itself.

---

### 4. Outro Placement

Ending the video at minute 12–15 with a winner announcement followed by an outro is fine, but the outro should be brief (30–60 seconds). Long outros with extensive sponsor mentions and subscribe calls can hurt average view duration as viewers click away.

**Recommendation:** Keep the outro tight. The winner announcement and celebration should be the emotional climax, with the subscribe/social call to action kept to 15–20 seconds after.

---

## Summary

The main structural issues are: too much setup before the challenge begins, and the challenge itself starting too late in the video. Viewers come to see the challenge, so getting to it faster — while still providing enough context — will improve retention throughout the video.
