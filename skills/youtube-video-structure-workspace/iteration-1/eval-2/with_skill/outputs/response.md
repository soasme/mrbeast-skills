# Structural Review: Submitted Video Outline

## The Outline Under Review

| Segment | Time |
|---------|------|
| Introduction, explain rules, thank sponsors | 0–2 min |
| Show the set being built | 2–5 min |
| Contestants arrive and interview each one | 5–8 min |
| First challenge begins | 8–12 min |
| Winner announced and outro | 12–15 min |

---

## Structural Problems That Will Hurt Retention

### Problem 1: The Hook Is Broken (Minutes 0–2)

**The mistake:** Starting with a rule explanation and sponsor read.

This is the highest-risk 60 seconds of the entire video. Roughly 30–40% of viewers who clicked will leave in the first minute if they aren't immediately shown what they came for. A rule explanation tells the viewer what they're about to watch instead of showing it. The sponsor mention is even worse — it signals "content hasn't started yet."

**Effect:** Mass click-off before the video even begins. No hook, no viewer. The thumbnail made a promise — the first 15 seconds must deliver on it visually. If the thumbnail shows the challenge happening, the video must open on the challenge happening.

**Fix:** Cut the rule explanation entirely from the opening. Open on the most visually impressive, highest-energy moment — the challenge set fully built, contestants on deck, stakes visible. Rules can be communicated in 10 seconds through action and on-screen text (not a 2-minute monologue). Move the sponsor to minute 3–4, after viewer investment is established.

---

### Problem 2: Set-Building Content is Not Hook Content (Minutes 2–5)

**The mistake:** Dedicating 3 minutes to showing the set being built.

Set-building content is B-roll, not story. At minute 2, the viewer doesn't yet have emotional investment in any person or outcome. Watching construction equipment and production crew at work is not compelling in the pre-investment window. This is the kind of content that belongs in a behind-the-scenes upload or a vlog — not in the first 5 minutes of a challenge video.

**Effect:** Viewer boredom peak hits around minute 3 with no re-engagement present. This is where the retention graph will show a steep cliff.

**Fix:** Replace this entire section with the execution starting. By minute 2, contestants should already be competing. The set can be established visually in 3–5 seconds of wide-shot b-roll.

---

### Problem 3: Eight-Minute Delay Before the Challenge (Minutes 5–8)

**The mistake:** Interviewing contestants before any action has happened.

The challenge doesn't begin until minute 8. That means 8 full minutes of setup before the thing the thumbnail promised actually starts. At this point, viewers have no reason to care about any contestant yet because they haven't seen them do anything. Talking-head interviews without stakes are not engaging.

**Effect:** The majority of viewers who haven't already left will leave here. YouTube's algorithm will see low AVD and stop recommending this video.

**Fix:** Interviews should happen after viewers have already seen contestants in action. Introduce characters through their behavior in the competition, not through a pre-game sit-down. Any exposition needed can be delivered in 15–20 second cuts between action scenes.

---

### Problem 4: The Challenge Starts at Minute 8 — Way Too Late

**The mistake:** The core promise of the video (the challenge) doesn't begin until minute 8 in a 15-minute video. That's more than halfway through.

**Effect:** Most viewers have clicked away before the actual content begins. By minute 8, only the most loyal fans remain — and those viewers didn't need 8 minutes of warmup.

**Fix:** The challenge must begin by minute 1–2 at the absolute latest. The hook-transition-build framework requires execution to be visible within the first 60 seconds. Viewers click on a challenge video to see the challenge, not to be prepared for the challenge.

---

### Problem 5: No Re-Engagements Anywhere

**The mistake:** There are no planned spectacle moments or story pivots at the 3-minute or 6-minute marks.

Even if the overall structure were improved, a flat 15 minutes with no deliberate re-engagement moments will bleed viewers steadily. The 3-minute mark is a predictable exit point — viewers who haven't been surprised yet will click away. The 6-minute mark is where emotionally invested viewers need a story escalation to carry them through the back half.

**Fix:**
- **3-minute re-engagement:** Insert a high-spectacle, instantly-understood moment — something jaw-dropping tied to the challenge. Example: a surprise twist announced mid-challenge, a visual event that raises stakes.
- **6-minute re-engagement:** A story-critical development that pushes the narrative into the back half. Example: a shocking elimination, a rule change, a challenge within the challenge.

---

### Problem 6: Winner Shouldn't Be Signaled Early — Only Revealed at the Very End

**The mistake:** The structure signals the video is ending at minute 12 by labeling the segment "Winner announced and outro."

**Effect:** When viewers sense a video is wrapping up, they stop watching. They've already gotten the emotional payoff in their minds. The outro is now dead weight and viewers skip past or click away — killing the last 3 minutes of AVD.

**Fix:** Never frame any segment as "the outro" in your planning. The winner reveal should be the absolute last thing that happens. Cut immediately after the winner is revealed — no outro, no "thanks for watching," no subscribe plea. End abruptly on the highest emotional moment. The winner being handed the prize check is the last frame. Then cut to black.

---

## Recommended Restructure

| Segment | Time |
|---------|------|
| Challenge visuals confirm thumbnail + stakes established | 0–1 min |
| Challenge underway, early compression of key moments | 1–3 min |
| **3-minute re-engagement:** major twist or spectacle | ~3 min |
| Character development through competition action | 3–6 min |
| **6-minute re-engagement:** story-critical development that raises stakes | ~6 min |
| Back half: drama, slow burn, emotional investment payoff | 6–12 min |
| Final push to winner reveal | 12–14:30 min |
| Winner revealed — abrupt cut after payoff | 14:30–15 min |

Sponsors should be placed at minute 3–4 or 8–9 (mid-lull), never in the first 2 minutes.
